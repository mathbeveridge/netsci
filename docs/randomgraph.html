Section 4.1 Erdos-Renyi Random Graph

Recall that a finite probability space \((\Omega, \P)\) consists of a finite set \(\Omega\) and a function \(\P: \mathcal{P}(\Omega) \rightarrow [0,1]\) such that

  • \(\displaystyle \P(\emptyset) = 0\)

  • \(\displaystyle \P(\Omega) = 1\)

  • For \(A,B \subset \Omega\text{,}\) if \(A \cap B = \emptyset\) then \(\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B)\text{.}\) \(\P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_k) \leq \sum_{i=1}^k \P(A_i)\) for any events \(A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_k \subset \Omega\text{.}\)

We refer to \(\omega \in \Omega\) as an outcome, and we refer to \(A \subset \Omega\) as an event.

The random graph \(G(n,p)\) is a probability space where \(\Omega\) is the set of all possible graphs on \([n]\text{.}\) Note that the vertices are labeled, so the same graph structure appears once for each possible labeling. For every \(G \in \Omega\text{,}\) with \(k\) edges, we have

\begin{equation*} P(G) = p^k(1-p)^{{n \choose 2} -k}. \end{equation*}

Exercises Practice Problems

1. The Random Graph \(G(3,2/3)\).

  1. Here are the graphs in \(G(3, 2/3)\text{.}\) Write down their corresponding probabilities

  2. Find the probabilities of the following events

    1. \(G\) has exactly one edge

    2. \(G\) is connected

    3. Vertex 1 has degree \(k\) for \(k=0,1,2\text{.}\)

  3. Calculate \(\E[\deg(1)]\text{.}\)

2. \(G(n,p)\) with Exactly \(m\) Edges.

Pick a random graph \(G \in G(n,p)\text{.}\) Explain why the probability that \(G\) has exactly \(m\) edges is

\begin{equation*} P(m) = {{n \choose 2} \choose m} p^m (1-p)^{{n \choose 2} - m}. \end{equation*}
3. The Expected Number of Edges in \(G(n,p)\).

By evaluating the sum, prove that the expected number of edges in \(G\) is

\begin{equation*} \langle m \rangle = \sum_{m = 0}^{{n \choose 2}} m P(m) = {n \choose 2} p. \end{equation*}

Hint: Use \(N={n \choose 2}\) while you are simplifying to keep you focused on what is important.

4. The Degree Distribution of \(G(n,p)\).

Explain why the probability that vertex \(v\) of \(G(n,p)\) has degree \(k\) is given by

\begin{equation*} p_k = {n-1 \choose k} p^k (1-p)^{n-1-k}. \end{equation*}

In other words, the degree of a vertex is a random variable whose PMF is the binomial distribution. Therefore we say that \(G(n,p)\) has a "binomial degree distribution."

Notes about the binomial distribution:

  • See Exercise 2.2.2 for the basics about the binomial distribution.

  • See Exercise 2.2.5 to see how we can approximate the binomial distribution with a Poisson distribtuion. Namely, when \(k\) is very small, \(n\) is very large and \(p= c/n\text{,}\) we have

    \begin{equation*} p_k \approx e^{-c} \frac{c^k}{k!}. \end{equation*}

5. The Expected Degree of a Vertex.

Prove that the expected degree of vertex \(v_k\) is

\begin{equation*} \E[\deg(v_k)] = \langle v_k \rangle = (n-1)p. \end{equation*}
6. The Expected Number of Edges in \(G(n,p)\text{,}\) revisited.

Use your answer to the previous question and linearity of expectation

\begin{equation*} \E \left[ \sum_{i=1}^k {X_i}\right] = \sum_{i=1}^k \E \left[ {X_i}\right] \end{equation*}

to give an alternate proof that

\begin{equation*} \langle m \rangle ={n \choose 2} p. \end{equation*}
