Chapter 12 Erdos-Renyi Random Graph

Recall that a finite probability space \((\Omega, \P)\) consists of a finite set \(\Omega\) and a function \(\P: \mathcal{P}(\Omega) \rightarrow [0,1]\) such that

  • \(\displaystyle \P(\emptyset) = 0\)

  • \(\displaystyle \P(\Omega) = 1\)

  • For \(A,B \subset \Omega\text{,}\) if \(A \cap B = \emptyset\) then \(\P(A \cup B) = \P(A) + \P(B)\text{.}\) \(\P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_k) \leq \sum_{i=1}^k \P(A_i)\) for any events \(A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_k \subset \Omega\text{.}\)

We refer to \(\omega \in \Omega\) as an outcome, and we refer to \(A \subset \Omega\) as an event.

The random graph \(G(n,p)\) is a probability space where \(\Omega\) is the set of all possible graphs on \([n]\text{.}\) Note that the vertices are labeled, so the same graph structure appears once for each possible labeling. For every \(G \in \Omega\text{,}\) with \(k\) edges, we have

\begin{equation*} P(G) = p^k(1-p)^{{n \choose 2} -k}. \end{equation*}

Exercises Practice Problems

1. The Random Graph \(G(3,2/3)\).

  1. Here are the graphs in \(G(3, 2/3)\text{.}\) Write down their corresponding probabilities

    Solution.

  2. Find the probabilities of the following events

    1. \(G\) has exactly one edge Solution.

      \begin{equation*} 3 \cdot \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^2 \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) = \frac{2}{9} \end{equation*}

    2. \(G\) is connected Solution.

      \begin{equation*} 3 \cdot \left( \frac{1}{3} \right) \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) ^2 + \left( \frac{2}{3} \right)^3 = \frac{20}{27} \end{equation*}

    3. Vertex 1 has degree \(k\) for \(k=0,1,2\text{.}\) Solution.

      \begin{align*} \Pr(\deg(1)=0) &= \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^3 + \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^2 \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) = \frac{3}{27}\\ \Pr(\deg(1)=1) &= 2 \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)^2\left( \frac{2}{3} \right) + 2 \left( \frac{1}{3} \right) \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) ^2 = \frac{12}{27}\\ \Pr(\deg(1)=2) &= \left( \frac{1}{3} \right)\left( \frac{2}{3} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{2}{3} \right) ^3 = \frac{12}{27} \end{align*}

  3. Calculate \(\E[\deg(1)]\text{.}\) Solution.

    \begin{equation*} 0 \cdot \frac{3}{27} + 1 \cdot \frac{12}{27} + 2 \cdot \frac{12}{27} = \frac{36}{27} = \frac{4}{3} \end{equation*}

2. \(G(n,p)\) with Exactly \(m\) Edges.

Pick a random graph \(G \in G(n,p)\text{.}\) Explain why the probability that \(G\) has exactly \(m\) edges is

\begin{equation*} P(m) = {{n \choose 2} \choose m} p^m (1-p)^{{n \choose 2} - m}. \end{equation*}

Solution.

We must pick the \(m\) edges. These must be the only edges that appear: \(p^m\) says that those \(m\) edges are present and \((1-p)^{{n \choose 2} - m}\) says that the remaining edges are not present.

3. The Expected Number of Edges in \(G(n,p)\).

By evaluating the sum, prove that the expected number of edges in \(G\) is

\begin{equation*} \langle m \rangle = \sum_{m = 0}^{{n \choose 2}} m P(m) = {n \choose 2} p. \end{equation*}

Hint: Use \(N={n \choose 2}\) while you are simplifying to keep you focused on what is important. Solution.

Let \(N = {n \choose 2}\text{.}\) We have

\begin{align*} \langle m \rangle &= \sum_{m = 0}^{N} m {N \choose m} p^m (1-p)^{N - m}\\ &= \sum_{m = 1}^{N} m \frac{N!}{m! (N-m)!} p^m (1-p)^{N - m}\\ &= p N \sum_{m = 1}^{N} m \frac{(N-1)!}{(m-1)! (N-m)!} p^(m-1) (1-p)^{N - m}\\ &= pN (p + (1-p))^{N-1} \, = p N = p {n \choose 2}. \end{align*}

4. The Degree Distribution of \(G(n,p)\).

Explain why the probability that vertex \(v\) of \(G(n,p)\) has degree \(k\) is given by

\begin{equation*} p_k = {n-1 \choose k} p^k (1-p)^{n-1-k}. \end{equation*}

In other words, \(G(n,p)\) has a binomial degree distribution. You need to use the following fact: when \(k\) is fixed, \(n\) is large and \(p= c/n\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} p_k \approx e^{-c} \frac{c^k}{k!}. \end{equation*}

So we can approximate the degree distribution of \(G(n,p)\) with a Poisson distribution. Solution.

Look at a particular vertex \(v\text{.}\) We must choose the \(k\) neighbors. The edges between \(v\) and those neighbors must be present. The edges from \(v\) to the remaining vertices must be absent.

5. The Expected Degree of a Vertex.

Prove that the expected degree of vertex \(v_k\) is

\begin{equation*} \E[\deg(v_k)] = \langle v_k \rangle = (n-1)p. \end{equation*}

Solution.

\begin{align*} \E[\deg(v_k)] &= \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} i {n-1 \choose i } p^i (1-p)^{n-1-i}\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1)!}{(i-1)! (n-1-i)!} p^i (1-p)^{n-1-i}\\ &= (n-1)p \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \frac{(n-2)!}{(i-1)! (n-1-i)!} p^{i-1} (1-p)^{n-1-i}\\ &= (n-1)p \end{align*}

6. The Expected Number of Edges in \(G(n,p)\text{,}\) revisited.

Use your answer to the previous question and linearity of expectation

\begin{equation*} \E \left[ \sum_{i=1}^k {X_i}\right] = \sum_{i=1}^k \E \left[ {X_i}\right] \end{equation*}

to give an alternate proof that

\begin{equation*} \langle m \rangle ={n \choose 2} p. \end{equation*}

Solution.

\begin{equation*} \langle m \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \E \left[ \sum_v \deg(v) \right] = \frac{1}{2} \sum_v \E[\deg(v)] = \frac{1}{2} n(n-1) p = {n \choose 2} p \end{equation*}
