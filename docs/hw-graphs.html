Chapter 17 Exercises: Graphs and Digraphs

Exercises Homework Exercises

1. Sources and Sinks.

Given a directed graph (digraph) \(D=(V,E)\) is acyclic, a directed cycle of size \(k\) is a set of arcs \(\)(v_1, v_2), (v_2, v_3), \ldots (v_{k-1}, v_k), (v_k, v_1)\(\) such that \(v_1\) is the only vertex that repeats. An acyclic digraph \(D\) has no directed cycles for any \(k \geq 1\text{.}\) Prove that if \(D\) is an acyclic digraph, then \(D\) contains at least one source vertex \(i\) with \(\kin_i=0\) and at least one sink vertex \(j\) with \(\kout_j=0\text{.}\)

2. Digraph for a Function.

Let \(f: [n] \rightarrow [n]\) be any function from the set \([n] = \{ 1,2,3, \ldots, n \}\) to itself. We can use this function to create a digraph \(D=([n], E)\) where the set of arcs is

\begin{equation*} E = \{ (i, f(i)) : i \in [n] \}. \end{equation*}

Note that when \(f(i)=i\text{,}\) we get a loop (which is a directed cycle of length 1).

  1. Draw the digraph for the function \(f: [8] \rightarrow [8]\) given by

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|cccccccc} k & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 & 8 \\ \hline f(k) & 6 & 4 & 3 & 8 & 4 & 1 & 8 & 2 \end{array} \end{equation*}

  2. Explain why \(\kout_i=1\) for every vertex \(i\text{.}\)

  3. Prove that \(D\) consists of a collection of disjoint unicyclic digraphs, meaning that each weakly connected component contains exactly one directed cycle. (Hint: assume for the sake of contradiction that some weakly connected component has two directed cycles. There are two cases: (1) the cycles share one or more vertices and (2) the cycles are disjoint.)

3. Acyclic Tournaments.

An acyclic tournament is an orientation of a complete graph that contains no directed cycles.

  1. Prove that every triangle is transitive, meaning that if \((u,v)\) and \((v,w)\) are both arcs, then \((u,w)\) is also an arc.

  2. Prove that an acyclic tournament has a unique source vertex \(s\) (with \(\kin_s=0\)) and a unique sink vertex \(t\) (with \(\kout_t = 0\)).

  3. Use part (b) and induction on \(N\) to prove that there is a unique acyclic tournament structure on \(N\) vertices (up to isomorphism). Or equivalently, show that we can label the vertices of a transitive tournament on \(N\) vertices using labels \([N] = \{ 1,2, \ldots, N \}\) such that if \(i >j\) then \((i,j)\) is an arc in the tournament.

  4. A transitive tournament is a tournament in which every triangle is transitive. Prove that a transitive tournament is acyclic. (Hint: there is a nice poof by induction on \(k\) for the statement "\(D\) has no \(k\) cycles for \(3 \leq k \leq N\text{.}\)" But there are other proofs, too.)

4. Orientation of a Graph.

Let \(G=(V,E)\) be an undirected graph. Let \(D\) be an orientation of this graph (so \(D\) is a directed graph).

  1. Prove that if \(D\) is strongly connected, then every pair of vertices in \(G\) is connected by two edge-disjoint paths.

  2. The converse is also true: if every pair of vertices in \(G\) is connected by two edge-disjoint paths, then there is a strongly connected orientation \(D\) of the graph. Here is the sketch of an algorithm that orients the edges of \(G\) to obtain a strongly connected digraph.

    1. Start with any two vertices \(u,v\) and orient some edges so that they are strongly connected to one another. Let \(S = \{ u , v\}\text{.}\)

    2. Pick a vertex \(w \notin S\text{.}\) If \(w\) and \(v\) are not strongly connected, then orient some edges to make this happen. Add \(w\) to \(S\text{.}\)

    3. Repeat the previous step until \(S = V\text{,}\) which means that every vertex is strongly connected to \(v\text{.}\)

    4. If there any any non-oriented edges, give them an orientation.

Fill in the details of the algorithm by explaining how to "orient some edges" in steps i, ii and iv. Explain how to choose the edges to orient, and how to pick the orientation of each edge. Finally, justify why your algorithm results in a strongly connected digraph. Drawing pictures while you are thinking (and explaining) will really help!

5. Directed rooted trees.

Let \(G=(V,E)\) be a tree and let \(v \in V\text{.}\) To obtain a directed tree rooted at vertex \(v\text{,}\) we orient the edges so that they point away from the vertex \(v\text{.}\) In other words, there is a directed path from \(v\) to every other vertex in the tree.

Prove that if \(D = (V,E)\) is a strongly connected digraph, then for every \(v \in V\text{,}\) the digraph contains a directed tree \(T = (V_T, E_T)\) rooted at \(v\) such that \(V_T = V\text{.}\)

(Note: A subgraph that includes every vertex of the original graph is called a spanning subgraph. Spanning trees (of graphs) and spanning directed trees (of digraphs) have many important applications.)

6. The Digraph of SCCs is Acyclic.

Let \(D=(V,E)\) be a weakly connected digraph with strongly connected components \(S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_k\text{.}\) Create a new digraph \(G\) with vertices \(V(G) = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_k \}\) where arc \((s_i,s_j) \in E(G)\) if and only if there is at least one arc from \(S_i\) to \(S_j\) in the original digraph \(D\text{.}\)

Prove that \(G\) is an acyclic digraph. (See the "Sources and Sinks" problem above for the definition of an acyclic digraph.)
