Section 2.3 Hubs and Authorities

At this point, we have seen three different "feedback centralities" that identify important vertices: eigenvector centrality, Katz centrality and PageRank centrality. Each of them can be phrased as dominant eigenvalue problem.

Today, we will talk about the HITS (Hyperlink-Induced Topic Search) algorithm, which takes a slightly different approach. Eigenvectors will still be the key to understanding this feedback centrality algorithm. The core idea of HITS is that vertices can play two different roles in the network:

  • Authorities: contain useful information (eg restaurant, college, newspaper)

  • Hubs: pages that link to authorities (eg Yelp, college admission consultant, news aggregator)

The point here is that authorities are in competition with one another, so they have no incentive to link to one another. Instead, hubs act as the aggregators, being expert consumers of the information that the authorities provide. Finally, these are roles, so it is possible for a vertex to be both an authority and a hub at the same time.

Therefore, each vertex will get two scores instead of one: an authority score and a hub score. Therefore, we will replace the one-step recursive mantra "important vertices have important neighbors" with the following two-step recursive mantra:

  • If \(v\) has a high authority score, then \(v\) has in-neighbors with high hub scores.

  • If \(v\) has a high hub score, then \(v\) has out-neighbor with high authority scores.

The HITS algorithm works as follows

  • Initialize hub score \(\leftarrow\) 1.

  • Repeat:

    • Update the authority score \(\leftarrow\) sum on in-neighbors' (new) hub scores.

    • Update the hub score \(\leftarrow \) sum of out-neighbors' authority scores.

    • Normalize the scores so that each maximum is 1.

Here is what it looks like on a bipartite directed graph where all the edges point from set \(X\) to set \(Y\text{.}\) In this case, the authority scores in \(X\) will always be 0, and the hub scores in \(Y\) will always be 0. So we only show the hub scores for \(X\) and the authority scores for \(Y\text{.}\) We also skip the normalization.

Let's give a mathematical description of this rule. The HITS algorithm's double recursion updates the hub score \(h_i\) and the authority score \(a_i\) for each vertex. The recursion is:

  • Initially, \(h_i(0) = 1\) for every vertex \(i\)

  • Repeat until convergence for \(t \geq 1\text{:}\)

    • For every vertex \({i}\text{,}\) update \(\displaystyle{a_i(t) = \sum_{j \rightarrow i} h_j(t-1)}\)

    • For every vertex \(j\text{,}\) update \(\displaystyle{h_j(t) = \sum_{j \rightarrow i} a_i(t)}\)

    • Normalize both values.

2. HITS Update Rules.

With the previous problem in mind, write each of the update rules as a matrix equation using the adjacency matrix \(A\) and the column vectors

\begin{equation*} \ba(t) =\begin{bmatrix} a_1(t) \\ \vdots \\ a_n(t) \end{bmatrix} \mbox{ and } \bh(t) = \begin{bmatrix} h_1(t) \\ \vdots \\ h_n(t) \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

(Hint: you will need to use \(A^{\top}\) for one of your equations.) Check that your matrix equations are correct for the example from Problem 1. Solution.

Our recursive update rule is

\begin{align*} \bh(t) &= A \, \ba(t-1),\\ \ba(t) &= A^{\top} \, \bh(t). \end{align*}

Row \(i\) of \(A\) contains the out-neighbors of vertex \(i\text{.}\) Row \(i\) of \(A^{\top}\) contains the in-neighbors of \(i\text{.}\)

3. HITS Matrix Equations.

Using your answer in question 2, you can now write down two matrix recurrences for \(t \geq 2\text{:}\)

  • A matrix equation for finding \(\ba(t)\) directly from \(\ba(t-1)\text{.}\)

  • A matrix equation for finding \(\bh(t)\) directly from \(\bh(t-1)\text{.}\)

Solution.

We have

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{lllll} \bh(t) &=& A \ba(t-1) &=& \, A \, A^{\top} \, \bh(t), \\ \ba(t) &=& A^{\top} \bh(t) &=& A^{\top} \, A \, \ba(t-1) \end{array} \end{equation*}

which we can write more compactly as

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{lllll} \bh(t) &=& A \, A^{\top} \, \bh(t-1), \\ \ba(t) &=& A^{\top} \, A \, \ba(t-1). \end{array} \end{equation*}

4. HITS Eigensystems.

After convergence, our update rule becomes \(\ba \rightarrow c_1 \ba\) and \(\bh \rightarrow c_2 \bh\text{.}\) (Recall that HITS performs a renormalization at each step to deal with \(c_1, c_2\text{.}\)) So after convergence, your matrix equations become statements about eigenvectors and eigenvalues. With that in mind:

  1. How are \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) related to the eigenvalues of your matrices in question 2?

  2. How do \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) relate to each other?

  3. What is the (linear algebraic) relationship between the authority vector \(\ba\) and the hub vector \(\bh\text{?}\)

For inspiration you should look back at our discussion of other algebraic centralities. Solution.

For each of the matrix equations above, as \(t \rightarrow \infty\text{,}\) we converge to a vector in the direction of the largest eigenvector. In other words, the hub score \(\bh\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} A \, A^{\top} \, \bh= \lambda \bh \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda\) is the largest eigenvalue of \(A \, A^{\top}\text{.}\) Meanwhile, the authority score \(\ba\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} A^{\top} \, A \, \ba= \mu \ba \end{equation*}

where \(\mu\) is the largest eigenvalue of \(A^{\top} \,A\text{.}\)

Here's a cool connection: a couple of standard linear algebra results will show us that \(\lambda=\mu\text{.}\) Recall from your linear algebra class

  • For any matrix \(M\text{,}\) we have \(\det(M) = \det(M^{\top})\text{.}\)

  • The eigenvalues of a matrix \(B\) are the solutions to

    \begin{equation*} \det(B - \mu I) = 0 \end{equation*}

Therefore, \(B\) and \(B^{\top}\) have the same eigenvalues (though they usually have different eigenvectors). By observing that \((A A^{\top})^{\top} = A^{\top} A,\) we conclude that \(\lambda = \mu\text{.}\)

Let's go a step further. Taking the transpose of the equation \(A^{\top} \, A \, \ba= \mu \ba\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} \ba^{\top} A \, A^{\top} = \lambda \ba^{\top}. \end{equation*}

In other words, the row vector \(\ba^{\top}\) is a left eigenvector of the matrix \(A \, A^{\top}\) for the largest eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\) Meanwhile,

\begin{equation*} A \, A^{\top} \, \bh= \lambda \bh \end{equation*}

says that the column vector \(\bh\) is a right eigenvector for the same eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\)

Every \(n \times n\) matrix has both left eigenvectors and right eigenvectors. You might be more comfortable with left eigenvectors because we tend to focus on column vectors. But matrix transposition is a simple operation that swaps the roles of rows and vectors. So any result about column vectors can be turned into an equivalent statement about row vectors.
