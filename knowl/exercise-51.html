Exercise 3. The Normalizing Constant for Exponent \(\alpha\).

  1. For many real-world networks, the power law behavior doesn't hold for small \(k\text{.}\) In this case, we restrict our attention to vertices of degree at least \(k_{\min}\text{.}\) We then deal with the probability mass function for a truncated power law. Given \(\alpha\) and \(k \geq k_{\min}\) this PMF is given by

    \begin{equation*} p(k) = \frac{ k^{-\alpha}}{\zeta(\alpha, k_{\min})} \end{equation*}

    where

    \begin{equation*} \zeta(x, k_{\min}) = \sum_{k=k_{\min}}^{\infty} k^{-x} \end{equation*}

    is the generalized Riemann zeta function.

    Dealing with the Riemann zeta function is unwieldy. In practice, we approximate this leading constant by the corresponding integral. Evaluate the following integral in order to find the approximate value for \(\zeta(\alpha, k_{\min})\text{:}\)

    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k= k_{\min}}^{\infty} k^{-\alpha} \approx \int_{x= k_{\min}}^{\infty} x^{-\alpha} \, dx. \end{equation*}

  2. Find the corresponding approximate expression for the power law PMF \(p(x)\) using your constant from (b). Simplify so that \(\alpha\) only appears once in an exponent.
