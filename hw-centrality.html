Chapter 18 Exercises: Centrality Measures

Exercises 18.1 Homework Exercises

1. Weakly increasing centrality measures.

  1. For each of our basic centrality measures (degree centrality, closeness centrality, betweenness centrality), prove that if you add the edge \((u,v)\) to \(G\) then the centrality of vertex \(u\) does not decrease. (Mathematically, we say that the centrality of \(u\) is weakly increasing for edge additions at \(u\text{.}\))

  2. Which of these centrality measures are weakly increasing at every vertex \(w\) when you add a particular edge \((u,v)\text{?}\) For those that are weakly increasing, give a proof. For those that are not weakly increasing, give an example where adding the edge \((u,v)\) decreases the centrality at some other vertex \(w\text{.}\)

2. Closeness Centrality on a Tree.

Let \(G\) be an undirected tree. Removing the edge \((u,v)\) splits the graph into two disjoint trees \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) where \(u \in V(G_1)\) and \(v \in V(G_2)\text{.}\) Suppose that \(G_1\) has \(N_1\) vertices and \(G_2\) has \(N_2\) vertices. Prove that the (un-normalized) closeness centralities of \(u\) and \(v\) satisfy

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{c_u^C} + N_1 = \frac{1}{c_v^C} + N_2. \end{equation*}
3. Betweenness Centrality on a Tree.

Let \(G\) be an undirected tree on \(N\) vertices. Let \(v\) be a vertex with degree \(k_v = k\text{.}\) Removing the vertex \(v\) splits the tree into \(k \geq 2\) disjoint trees \(G_1,G_2, \ldots, G_k\text{.}\) Suppose that tree \(G_i\) contains \(N_i\) vertices for \(1 \leq i \leq k\text{.}\) Show that the (un-normalized) betweenness centrality of \(v\) satisfies

\begin{equation*} c_v^B = (N-1)^2 - \sum_{i=1}^k N_i^2 \end{equation*}
4. Counting Lazy Random Walks.

In a lazy walk on a directed graph, at each step, we can either stay at the current vertex or move to an adjacent vertex. So a lazy walk of length \(k\) is a sequence of vertices \(v_0, v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k\) where for \(1 \leq i \leq k\text{,}\) we either have \(v_i = v_{i-1}\) or \((v_{i-1}, v_i)\) is an arc of \(G\text{.}\) Let \(A\) be the adjacency matrix of a directed graph \(G\text{,}\) so that \(a_{ij}\) is 1 if \((i,j)\) is an arc and is 0 otherwise. Using induction on \(k \geq 0\text{,}\) prove that the \(ij\)th entry of \((I+A)^k\) counts the number of lazy walks of length \(k\) from vertex \(i\) to vertex \(j\text{.}\)

5. Katz centrality as \(\alpha \rightarrow 1/\kappa_1\).

Let \(G\) be a simple undirected graph with \(n \times n\) adjacency matrix \(A\text{.}\) Since \(G\) is undirected, its adjacency matrix is symmetric: \(A = A^{\top}\text{.}\) Therefore, the Katz centrality \(\bx \in \R^n\) is limiting value of the Katz recursion

\begin{align*} \bx_1 &= \bone\\ \bx_t &= \alpha A \bx_{t-1} + \bone \mbox{for } t \geq 2 \end{align*}

for some choice of \(\alpha\text{.}\) Let \(\kappa_1, \ldots \kappa_n\) be the eigenvalues of \(A\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(\bu_1, \bu_2, \ldots, \bu_n\text{.}\) By the Frobenius-Perron Theorem, \(\kappa_1 > | \kappa_i |\) for \(2 \leq i \leq n\text{,}\) and the eigenvectors form a basis for \(\R^n\text{.}\) Note that \(\bu_1\) is the eigenvector centrality of \(G\text{.}\) You will show that as \(\alpha \rightarrow 1/\kappa_1\text{,}\) the Katz centrality becomes proportional to eigenvector centrality.

  1. Pick any eigenvector \(\bu_i\) and consider the recursive process

    \begin{align*} \by_{i,1} &= \bu_i\\ \by_{i,t} &= \alpha A \by_{i,t-1} + \bu_i \mbox{for } t \geq 2. \end{align*}

    Use induction on \(t \geq 1\) to prove that

    \begin{equation*} \by_{i,t} = \left( 1 + \alpha \kappa_i + \alpha^2 {\kappa_i^2} + \cdots + \alpha^{t-1} {\kappa_i^{t-1}} \right) \bu_i. \end{equation*}

  2. Pick our constant \(\alpha > 0 \) such that

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{ \kappa_1} < \alpha < \frac{1}{|\kappa_i|} \end{equation*}

    for \(2 \leq i \leq n\text{.}\) What can you say about

    \begin{equation*} \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} \by_{1,t} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} \by_{i,t} \end{equation*}

    for \(2 \leq i \leq n\text{?}\) You will need to use one (or both) of the following facts about geometric sums and geometric series:

    \begin{equation*} 1 + r + r^2 + r^3 + \cdots + r^t = \sum_{k=0}^{t} r^k = \frac{r^{t+1} -1}{r-1} \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} 1 + r + r^2 + r^3 + \cdots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \left\{ \begin{array}{cl} \frac{1}{1-r} & \mbox{if } |r| < 1, \\ \infty & \mbox{otherwise}. \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

  3. We know that the eignvectors \(\bu_1, \ldots, \bu_n\) form a basis for \(\R^n\) (thanks, Frobenius-Perron!). In particular, we have

    \begin{equation*} \bone = c_1 \bu_1 + c_2 \bu_2 + \cdots + c_n \bu_n \end{equation*}

    for some choice of \(c_1, \ldots, c_n \in \R\text{.}\) Let us assume that \(c_1 \neq 0\text{.}\) Use parts (a) and (b) to show that the limiting value

    \begin{equation*} \bx = \alpha A \bx + \bone \end{equation*}

    of the Katz recursion is basically proportional to the eigenvector centrality \(\bu_1\text{.}\)

6. Katz centrality as \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\).

Now let's talk about what happens to the Katz centrality as \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\text{.}\) You will show that for very small \(\alpha\text{,}\) the Katz centrality gives a vertex ranking that is equivalent to degree centrality, meaning that the order of the ranking is the same as the degree centrality ranking. This time, we will start with the following equation, which is satisfied by the limiting solution to the Katz recursion:

\begin{equation*} \bx = \alpha A \bx + \bone. \end{equation*}

  1. Explain why \(A \bone < n \bone\text{.}\)

  2. If \(B\) is an \(n \times n\) matrix and suppose that \((I-B)\) is invertible, then we know that

    \begin{equation*} (I-B)^{-1} = \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} B^j = I + B + B^2 + \cdots + B^j + \cdots. \end{equation*}

    Use this fact to show that

    \begin{equation*} \bx = \bone + \alpha A \bone + \alpha^2 A^2 \bone + \cdots + \alpha^s A^s \bone + \cdots \end{equation*}

  3. Next, let \(\alpha = 1/m\) where \(m > 0\) is a very large number (to be determined later). Use parts (a) and (b) to show that

    \begin{equation*} \bx = \bone + \frac{1}{m} A \bone + \bb \end{equation*}

    where \(\bb\) is a vector satisfying

    \begin{equation*} | \bb | < \frac{n}{m (m-1)} \bone. \end{equation*}

  4. Finally, take \(m = n^2\) and look at \(\bx - \bone\text{.}\) Explain why ordering of the vertices by the entries in \(\bx - \bone\) gives the same ranking as degree centrality (as long as \(n \geq 2\)). A similar argument holds for any \(m > n^2\text{,}\) so as \(\alpha \rightarrow 0\text{,}\) the ranking is the same as the degree centrality ranking.

7. A PageRank Variant.
\begin{equation*} \bx = \alpha P^{\top} \bx + \frac{(1 - \alpha)}{n} \bone \end{equation*}

where \(P\) is the transition matrix for a random walk on the network. In this problem, you will consider a variant of this equation. Let \(S\) be any fixed set of \(m\) vertices and let \(\overline{S}\) be the \(n-m\) remaining vertices. Given a fixed (constant) vector \(\bb_S \in \R^m\) on the set \(S\text{,}\) we want to solve

\begin{equation*} \left[ \begin{array}{c} \bb_S \\ \bx_{\overline{S}} \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} I_{S,S} & \bzero_{S,\overline{S}} \\ \alpha P_{S,\overline{S}}^{\top} & \alpha P_{\overline{S}, \overline{S}}^{\top} \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} \bb_S \\ \bx_{\overline{S}} \end{array} \right] + \frac{(1 - \alpha)}{| \overline{S}|} \left[ \begin{array}{c} \bzero_S \\ \bone_{\overline{S}} \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}

for the unknown vector \(\bx_{\overline{S}} \in \R^{n-m}\) where

  • \(I_{S,S}\) is an \(m \times m\) identity matrix

  • \(\bzero_{S, \overline{S}}\) is an \(m \times (n-m)\) zero matrix.

  • \(P_{S,\overline{S}}\) is the \(m \times (n-m)\) matrix describing the transition probabilities from \(S\) to \(\overline{S}\)

  • \(P_{\overline{S},\overline{S}}\) is the \((n-m) \times (n-m)\) matrix describing the transition probabilities from \(\overline{S}\) to \(\overline{S}\)

  • \(\bzero_S\) is the \(m\)-dimensional all-zeros vector

  • \(\bone_{\overline{S}}\) is the \((n-m)\)-dimensional all-ones vector

  • \(\alpha \in [0,1]\text{.}\)

Given what you know about (standard) PageRank, interpret the "update rule" that this equation calculates. Then describe a web searching example where using this equation produces a helpful ranking.

8. Hubs and Authorities as an Eigenvector.

Let \(A\) be the adjacency matrix of network \(G\text{.}\) Define the \(2n \times 2n\) matrix

\begin{equation*} B = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 0 & A \\ A^{\top} & 0 \end{array} \right]. \end{equation*}

Let \(\bv = (v_1, \ldots, v_{2n})^{\top}\) be the right eigenvector for the largest eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of \(B^2\text{,}\) that is \(B^2 \bv = \lambda \bv.\) Show that \(\bh = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)^{\top}\) and \(\ba = (v_{n+1}, \ldots, v_{2n})^{\top}\) where \(\bh\) is the vector of hub scores of \(G\) and \(\ba\) is the vector of authority scores of \(G\text{.}\)

9. Symmetric Matrices have Orthogonal Eigenvectors.

In this problem, you will prove part of the Perron-Frobenius Theorem. Let \(\bx, \by \in \R^n\text{.}\) Recall that the dot product is defined as \(\bx \cdot \by = \bx^{\top} \by\text{,}\) and that \(\bx\) and \(\by\) are orthogonal when \(\bx \cdot \by = 0\text{.}\)

  1. Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix. Prove that \((A\bx) \cdot \by = \bx \cdot A^{\top} \by\text{.}\)

  2. Let \(A\) be a symmetric matrix, so that \(A = A^{\top}\text{.}\) Let \(\lambda\) and \(\mu\) be distinct nonzero eigenvalues with corresponding (right) eigenvectors \(\bu\) and \(\bv\text{,}\) so that \(A \bu = \lambda \bu\) and \(A \bv = \lambda \bv\text{.}\) Prove that \((A \bu) \cdot \bv =0\text{.}\)

  3. Explain why part (b) ensures that the eigenvectors \(\bu\) and \(\bv\) are orthogonal.

10. Symmetric Matrices have \(\kappa_1 \geq 1\).

Let \(A\) be the adjacency matrix for a connected, undirected and aperiodic graph. Recall that the Frobenius-Perron Theorem guarantees that the symmetric matrix \(A\) has real eigenvalues \(\kappa_1 \geq \kappa_2 \geq \cdots \geq \kappa_n\) with corresponding orthonormal eigenvectors \(\bu_1, \bu_2, \ldots , \bu_n\text{.}\) Furthermore

  • The eigenvalue \(\kappa_1\) has multiplicity one, and \(\kappa_1 > | \kappa_i |\) for \(2 \leq i \leq n\text{.}\)

  • The corresponding eigenvector \(\bu_1\) has non-negative entries.

In this problem, you will show that when \(n \geq 3\text{,}\) we have \(\kappa_1 \geq 1\text{.}\)

  1. Let \(\bx = A \bone\text{.}\) Explain explain why \(\bx \geq \bone\text{,}\) (meaning \(x_i \geq 1\) for all \(i\)), and explain why there is at least one \(j\) such that \(x_j > 1\text{.}\)

  2. The orthonormal eigenvectors \(\bu_1, \bu_2, \ldots , \bu_n\) form a basis for \(\R^n\text{.}\) So there exist \(c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n\) such that

    \begin{equation*} \bone = c_1 \bu_1 + c_2 \bu_2 + \cdots + c_n \bu_n. \end{equation*}

    Consider

    \begin{equation*} A \bone = A(c_1 \bu_1 + c_2 \bu_2 + \cdots + c_n \bu_n). \end{equation*}

    Explain why \(\kappa_1 < 1\) leads to a contradiction to your observations in part (a).
