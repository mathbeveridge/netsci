Chapter 3 A4 Random Walks

A random walk starting at vertex \(v_0\) proceeds as follows. At each time \(t \geq 1\text{,}\) pick a random neighbor of \(v_{t-1}\) and walk to it. In other words, \(v_t\) is chosen uniformly from the out-neighbors of \(v_{t-1}\text{.}\) Using conditional probability notation, we have

\begin{equation*} P_{ij} = \Pr (v_t=j \mid v_{t-1} = i ) = \left\{ \begin{array}{cc} \displaystyle{\frac{1}{k_i^{\mathrm{out}}}} & \mbox{if } ij \in E, \\ 0 & \mbox{otherwise}. \end{array} \right. \end{equation*}

The transition matrix \(P = ( P_{ij})\) collects the transition probabilities. The \(i\)th row contains all the transition probabilities starting at vertex \(i\text{.}\) Finally, we can write our transition matrix as

\begin{equation*} P = A D^{-1} \end{equation*}

where \(D\) is the diagonal matrix with \(D_{ii} = \max \left\{ k_i^{\mathrm{out}}, 1 \right\}\) and \(A\) is the adjacency matrix.

Exercises 3.1 Practice Problems

1. Random Walk on an Undirected Graph.

  1. Find the transition matrix \(P\) of the following undirected graph. Hint: construct \(P\) row by row.

  2. Show that the all-ones vector \(\bone\) is a right eigenvector for eigenvalue \(\lambda=1\text{.}\) That is, confirm that \(P \bone = \bone.\) What is the meaning of this equation?

2. Random Walk on a Directed Graph.

  1. Find the transition matrix \(P\) of the following directed graph. Hint: construct \(P\) row by row.

  2. Confirm that \(P \bone = \bone.\) What is the meaning of this equation?

3. Long Term Behavior of a Random Walk of an Undirected Graph.

Consider the undirected graph from problem 1. In you mind, repeatedly simulate a random walk starting from vertex 1. Follow this walk for 15 steps. Where are you most likely to end up? Least likely? How do your observations relate to (1) the distance of the vertex from vertex 1 and (2) the degree of the vertex?

4. Long Term Behavior of a Random Walk on Directed Graph.

Now simulate some random walks on the directed graph from problem 2. This time, start your walk at vertex 0. You should see a few different kinds of behavior for this directed graph. Describe what you observe.

5. An Eigenvector for an Undirected Graph.

Let's return to the undirected graph in problems 1 and 3. Check that the row vector

\begin{equation*} \bv = \displaystyle{ \left( \frac{3}{15}, \frac{3}{15}, \frac{2}{15}, \frac{3}{15}, \frac{3}{15}, \frac{1}{15} \right)} \end{equation*}

satisfies \(\bv \,P = \bv\text{.}\) We say that "\(\bv\) is a left eigenvector of the transition matrix \(P\) for eigenvalue \(\lambda=1\text{.}\)" Can you see where these weights come from? How does that relate to your observations in problem 3? (Perhaps you should do the check that \(\bv P = \bv\) on the other side of the page, where you wrote down \(P\text{.}\))

6. An Eigenvector for a Directed Graph.

Now let's get back to the directed graph in problems 2 and 4. Check that the row vector

\begin{equation*} \left( 0,0,0,0,\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2} \right) \end{equation*}

satisfies \(\bv \, P = \bv.\) How does this relate to the behavior you saw in problem 4? What behavior doesn't this capture?
