Exercises 1.6 Practice Problems

1.

Binomial Random Variable. Flip a weighted coin \(n\) times, where \(p\) is the probability of obtaining heads, and \(1-p\) is the probability of obtaining tails. Define \(X\) to be the number of heads in the \(n\) tosses. This is the binomial random variable with parameters \(n\) and \(p\).

  1. Give a combinatorial argument for why the PMF of the binomial random variable is

    \begin{equation*} p(k) = {n \choose k} p^k (1 - p)^{n-k}. \end{equation*}

  2. Use the binomial theorem

    \begin{equation*} (x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n {n \choose k} x^ky^{n-k} \end{equation*}

    to prove that \(p(k)\) is a PMF.

  3. Show that

    \begin{equation*} \E[X] = np. \end{equation*}

    (Hint: factor out an \(n\) and then reindex your sum.)

  4. It can be shown that

    \begin{equation*} \var(X) = np(1-p). \end{equation*}

    But you do not need to do this here. You need to use the fact that

    \begin{equation*} \E[X^2] = \E[X(X-1) + X] = \E[X(X-1)] + \E[X], \end{equation*}

    which follows from linearity of expectation.

2.

Geometric Random Variable. Instead of flipping our coin for a fixed number of times, we will flip it until we first encounter a heads. This experiment gives the geometric random variable \(Y\text{.}\)

  1. Give a combinatorial argument for why the PMF of the geometric random variable is

    \begin{equation*} p(k) = (1 - p)^{k-1} p. \end{equation*}

  2. Use the fact that

    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k = \frac{1}{1-x} \end{equation*}

    to prove that \(p(k)\) is a PMF.

  3. Show that

    \begin{equation*} \E[Y] = \frac{1}{p}. \end{equation*}

    Hint: take the derivative of both sides of the equality in part(b) to get a new identity.

  4. It can be shown that \(\)\displaystyle{\var(Y) = \frac{1-p}{p^2}}.\(\) But you do not need to prove this here. (You use the same trick as in problem 1(d) of writing \(\E[Y^2] = \E[Y(Y-1)] + E[Y]\text{.}\))

3.

Poisson Random Variable. The Poisson random variable \(Z\) for parameter \(\lambda > 0\) has PMF given by

\begin{equation*} p(k) = e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}. \end{equation*}

  1. Use the fact that

    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^k}{k!} = e^{x} \end{equation*}

    to prove that \(p(k)\) is a PMF.

  2. Show that

    \begin{equation*} \E[Z] = \lambda. \end{equation*}

  3. Show that

    \begin{equation*} \var[Z] = \lambda \end{equation*}

    as well! Again, we use the trick

    \begin{equation*} \E[Z^2] = \E[Z(Z-1)] + \E[Z]. \end{equation*}

  4. In this part, you will show that when \(n\) is very large and \(p\) is very small, the Poisson PMF for

    \begin{equation*} \lambda :=np \end{equation*}

    is a good approximation (for small \(k\)) of the binomial PMF with parameters \(n\) and \(p\text{.}\) We will fix \(k \in \Z_{\geq 0}\) to be constant and let \(n \rightarrow \infty\text{.}\) You will show that

    \begin{equation*} {n \choose k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \approx e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!} \mbox{if } k \ll n. \end{equation*}

    Use the following facts which hold for \(n \rightarrow \infty\) and any constants \(c,d \in \Z_{\geq 0}\text{.}\)

    \begin{equation*} \begin{array}{ccc} \displaystyle{\frac{n -c}{n} \rightarrow 1}, & \displaystyle{\left( 1 - \frac{c}{n} \right)^{-d} \rightarrow 1,} & \displaystyle{\left( 1 - \frac{c}{n} \right)^n \rightarrow e^{-c}.} \end{array} \end{equation*}
